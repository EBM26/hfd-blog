How Well Do You Know NSAIDs: A Quiz

How Well Do You Know NSAIDs: A Quiz

By Nicole Trelour, ND - August 16, 2017

NSAIDs, or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, are some of the most commonly used pain relievers in the entire country. Just how common are they? By some estimates, 30 million Americans take an NSAID every single day.

All of which means the odds are good that you've got NSAIDs in your medicine cabinet right now. Unfortunately, they may not be as safe as you may think. So it's important to know as much as possible about them and what you're putting in your body every time you take one.

Take this quick quiz and double-check your NSAID knowledge.

  1. Which one of these brands is NOT an NSAID?
    1. Advil
    2. Aleve
    3. Aspirin
    4. Tylenol

    Answer: D, Tylenol. Ibuprofen (Advil), naproxen (Aleve), and aspirin are all NSAIDs. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is not because it doesn't block the enzymes that contribute to inflammation. In other words, it's not an anti-inflammatory the way the others are.

  2. Most NSAIDs increase the risk of:
    1. Heart attack
    2. Stroke
    3. Internal bleeding
    4. Kidney damage
    5. All of the above
    6. None of the above

    Answer: E, all of the above. These increased risks are what make NSAIDs so dangerous. The risks of heart attack and stroke increase after just a few weeks' of taking most NSAIDs, and patients whose first heart attack was treated with non-aspirin NSAIDs are more likely to die within the first year than people who weren't treated with medication at all. As for the internal bleeding, that can come from the damage to your gastrointestinal system. And since NSAIDs reduce the amount of blood that reaches the kidneys, toxins can build up, causing damage.

  3. Even though it's an NSAID, aspirin works differently in your body.
    1. True
    2. False

    Answer: A, True. Since aspirin thins the blood, it can help prevent a heart attack, rather than increase the risk of having one. The other side effects are still real possibilities, though. For people at risk of a heart attack, the benefits of aspirin often outweigh the potential side effects of taking a low-dose aspirin. Talk to your doctor about your health and priorities.

  4. Which is NOT a common side effect of NSAIDs?
    1. Headaches
    2. Ringing in the ears
    3. Swelling in the legs
    4. Loss of bone density
    5. All of the above
    6. None of the above

    Answer: D, loss of bone density. While not as serious as the side effects we've already touched on, NSAIDs can indeed cause these other, annoying, side effects as well. If you experience any of these while you're taking an NSAID, it's not a coincidence!

  5. Prescription NSAIDs are the ones you really need to worry about. Over-the-counter ones are perfectly safe, so long as you follow the dosing recommendations.
    1. True
    2. False

    Answer: B, False. Even the OTC versions of NSAIDs, and even the recommended "safe" doses can cause serious side effects. In 2015, the FDA quietly increased the warnings against taking NSAIDs for any length of time, and started recommending they be taken for the shortest amount of time possible. All of the serious side effects can occur after taking NSAIDs for a few short weeks. It's vital to weigh the risks and benefits of taking NSAIDs, even for a little while.

Now, if you choose to take NSAIDs or not, you can talk to your doctor and make an educated, informed decision-and that's what matters most.
